Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu:

Machu Picchu was built by the Inca civilization, likely around the mid-15th century, during the reign of the Inca emperor Pachacuti. Its construction is estimated to have taken approximately 100 years, with the site serving as both a royal estate and a ceremonial center. The exact purpose of Machu Picchu remains a subject of debate among scholars. Some theories suggest that it served as a royal retreat or a religious sanctuary, while others propose that it had astronomical or agricultural significance. It's likely that Machu Picchu fulfilled multiple functions, including as a residence for the Inca elite, a center for religious rituals, and a site for agricultural experimentation.

History

Although local indigenous populations were aware of the existence of Machu Picchu, it remained largely unknown to the outside world until it was rediscovered by the American explorer Hiram Bingham in 1911. Bingham, along with a team of archaeologists and locals, uncovered the overgrown ruins and brought international attention to the site.

Places

Today, Machu Picchu is one of the most visited tourist attractions in the world, drawing millions of visitors each year to marvel at its breathtaking scenery and ancient ruins. Efforts have been made to manage tourism and preserve the site's fragile ecosystem and archaeological remains, including implementing visitor limits, conservation measures, and sustainable tourism practices.

Food and drinks

Mate de coca is a tea made from coca leaves, which are native to the Andean region. It's believed to have medicinal properties and is often consumed to alleviate symptoms of altitude sickness. Mate de coca is widely available in Cusco and is served hot or cold.
